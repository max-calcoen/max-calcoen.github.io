Graves' Disease

(hyperthyroidism)

Graves' disease is an autoimmune disorder that affects the thyroid gland, causing it to produce excessive amounts of thyroid hormones. This results in hyperthyroidism, which can cause a wide range of symptoms, including weight loss, fatigue, diarrhea, headaches, and visual disturbances like bitemporal hemianopsia.

Hyperparathyroidism

Hyperparathyroidism occurs when your parathyroid glands cause a high concentration of parathyroid hormone (PTH) in the bloodstream. PTH helps maintain the balance of calcium in the bloodstream and in tissues that require calcium for their function. This is especially important for nerve and muscle function, as well as bone health. There are two main types of Hyperparathyroidism, primary and secondary. Primary Hyperparathyroidism describes the enlargement of one or more parathyroid glands, resulting in high blood calcium levels which cause health problems. Secondary Hyperparathyroidism describes a condition where another disease causes low calcium levels and increased parathyroid hormone levels occur in order to regain normal calcium levels. This condition most frequently occurs after kidney disease and after certain intestinal surgeries or diseases.

Pituitary Adenoma

A Pituitary Adenoma is a type of tumor that affects the pituitary gland. They are the fourth most common brain tumor, and almost all are benign. The pituitary gland consists of the anterior and posterior pituitary glands, and is located at the base of the brain, below the hypothalamus. It communicates with the rest of the endocrine system, a network of glands and organs that make and release hormones to control bodily functions. The effect of the tumor depends on where it is located. Mary Keeper most likely has an anterior pituitary adenoma, as the hormone changes she experiences match those produced by the anterior pituitary gland.
